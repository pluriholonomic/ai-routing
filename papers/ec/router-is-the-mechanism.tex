\title{Information Congestion in AI Inference Routing:\\
Market-Share Elasticity and Mechanism Design}

\author{Tarun Chitra}
\affiliation{\institution{Gauntlet}\city{New York}\country{USA}}
\email{tarun@gauntlet.xyz}

\begin{abstract}
Open-weight models create a real-time market for partly substitutable execution:
harnesses originate token demand, routers buy execution, and providers sell
perishable compute.  We ask when price-sensitive routing makes adaptive pricing
an information externality.  If $k$ providers respond to signals of effective
rank $r_n\asymp n^\beta$, a simple congestion model has
$k^*\asymp n^{(1+\gamma\beta)/(1+\gamma)}$: the efficient adaptive set is a
vanishing fraction when $\beta<1$ and a constant fraction when $\beta=1$.
Public quotes show a large mechanism surface but do not identify the regime.
For GLM-5.2, three active undercutters in a 25-provider menu receive 18.62
percentage points of inverse-price shadow share from passive quotes; its rank
slope is 0.967 (block-bootstrap interval $[0.516,1.219]$), hence linear-
compatible.  Non-GLM active repricing is sparse.  Paid rule interventions are
promising but still accruing.  A heterogeneous-provider game then shows that
welfare-, revenue-, quality-, and viability-optimal router rules diverge.  We
derive score and capacity bounds and propose covariance-aware exposure caps.
All empirical claims are property tests; none identifies collusion or welfare.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Open weights separate a model from the firm that executes it.  Two providers
can serve the same model on the same prompt, making execution partly
commodity-like.  Yet tool calls, quantization, sampling, context limits,
latency, privacy, and hidden capacity keep the products from being perfectly
fungible.  This produces a new vertical market: a user delegates to a harness;
the harness chooses a model and possibly a router; the router aggregates token
demand and dispatches an eligible provider; and inference providers sell
perishable GPU-backed service.

The closest DeFi analogy is useful but incomplete.  A harness resembles a
wallet or intent originator, a router resembles a DEX aggregator, a model is a
common execution standard, and a provider resembles a liquidity provider.
Unlike an atomic swap, however, an inference request is normally assigned to a
single provider, may be retried serially, has stochastic quality, and cannot be
verified from price alone.  The router is therefore not merely an aggregator.
It is a delegated buyer, a multi-attribute scoring mechanism, and a reliability
intermediary.

This distinction matters empirically.  A public price is an invitation to route,
not a firm capacity commitment.  A documented score is not necessarily the
allocation law after private eligibility, quality, and fallback.  Provider cost
depends on whether capacity is owned, reserved, or bought in a spot market; the
same discount can reflect low marginal cost, subsidy, entry investment, or
predatory intent.  Consequently, price panels alone neither reveal flow nor
identify dumping, collusion, or welfare.

We build the paper around this identification problem.  Our evidence joins a
five-minute public menu panel, router-published enforcement aggregates, model-
author anchor prices, public quality fields, and randomized or assignment-first
owned requests.  The sources occupy different rungs of an identification
ladder.  Public menus reveal offers; public enforcement reveals router-side
state; mechanical shadow routing reveals the implications of an explicit rule;
owned requests reveal realized policy for one account and workload; and only
router or provider logs could reveal market-wide flow or private capacity.

The empirical results are property tests and rule counterfactuals.  On the
immutable 22 July revision, the public panel contains 3.18 million endpoint
observations and 3,274 snapshots.  GLM-5.2 has three pre-holdout active
undercutters in a median 25-provider menu.  Resetting those quotes to the author
benchmark transfers 18.62 percentage points of inverse-price shadow share back
to passive providers.  Yet the effective-rank ladder is linear-compatible at
1, 6, and 24 hour horizons, and a 70-market non-GLM census has zero active
undercutters in 90\% of markets.  The desired GLM-minority/non-GLM-linear
asymptotic contrast is therefore not an empirical finding.  Paid routing gives
a sharper intervention: in 15 complete blocks, explicitly requesting price
sorting raises cheapest-provider selection by 80 points relative to default
routing.  The preregistered duration and choice gates have not passed.

We make five mechanism-design contributions.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item We formulate separate user, router, provider, and planner objectives for
inference routing.  The formulation makes explicit why price-only, router-
revenue, and welfare objectives need not agree.
\item We give a conditional information-congestion theorem.  When provider
signals have sublinear effective rank, the efficient number of adaptive pricers
is sublinear even though each price cut can win share locally; with linear rank,
the efficient set is a constant fraction.
\item We prove a score--price equivalence and elasticity decomposition.  A
provider's relative non-price score is a price-equivalent subsidy or penalty;
its interaction with the price rule determines the return to undercutting.
\item For entropy-regularized routing, we prove an exact regret identity: using
a price-only score instead of the full generalized execution cost loses exactly
a scaled KL divergence.  A uniform score-error bound then gives a robust
approximation guarantee.
\item We prove a hidden-capacity impossibility result.  Two economies with the
same public menu can require opposite efficient allocations.  Every quote-only
router incurs at least half the cost gap in one of them.  Execution-contingent
capacity certificates are therefore an informational requirement, not merely a
feature request.
\end{enumerate}

Finally, we evaluate the theorem and mechanism in two structural simulations.
The fixed-$n$ integer grid recovers the predicted scaling exponents to within
0.006, but a marginal-preserving bandit intervention changes action correlation
without materially changing allocation.  A heterogeneous-provider game
recomputes bounded best responses under 108 router rules.  It exposes a large
conflict between router revenue and welfare and profitable pairwise joint
deviations even when unilateral grid regret is zero.  These are design screens,
not live-market welfare or conduct estimates.

The paper contributes to multidimensional procurement \cite{che1993},
two-sided platforms \cite{rochettirole2003}, pricing technology
\cite{brownmackay2023}, platform steering
\cite{johnsonrhodeswildenbeest2023}, and algorithmic interaction
\cite{hansenmisrapai2021}.  Its methodological position is deliberately
conservative: when market-wide flow and cost are hidden, theory should state
conditional guarantees and experiments should test mechanism properties before
assigning conduct.

\section{Market, Timing, and Objectives}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Participants and transaction}

A request has observable context $x$ and user value $v(x,q)$ for delivered
quality $q$.  A harness transforms the task and chooses a model-router pair.  The
router observes a private eligibility set $A(x)$, public or private endpoint
features, and possibly an ordered fallback list.  Provider $i$ posts input and
output token prices, summarized by an expected request price $p_i(x)$, and
delivers stochastic latency $L_i$, quality $q_i$, and success $y_i\in\{0,1\}$.

Providers differ in capacity technology.  An owned-capacity provider chooses
$K_i$ before demand, pays fixed cost $F_i(K_i)$, and may have low short-run
marginal cost.  A spot-dependent provider buys state-contingent capacity at
$c_i(x,z)$ and has less fixed exposure.  A model author can post a focal anchor
price.  A provider matching that anchor is ``non-strategic'' only with respect
to its displayed-price process; it can still optimize admission, quality, or
capacity.

Let $s_i$ be the probability that $i$ is attempted, including any position in a
declared fallback policy, and let $d_i=s_i\E[y_i\mid i,x]$ be delivered share.
Transfers cancel in static social surplus but affect entry and investment.  The
four objectives are
\begin{align}
U_i &= \E[(p_i-c_i(x,K_i,q_i))d_i-F_i(K_i)-\phi_i], \\
R &= \E[f(p_i,d_i)-C_{\rm retry}-C_{\rm SLA}], \\
V &= \E[v(x,q_i)y_i-p_i y_i-\lambda_L L_i-\lambda_F(1-y_i)], \\
W &= V+R+\sum_i U_i-\text{pure transfers},
\label{eq:objectives}
\end{align}
where $\phi_i$ includes penalties or collateral losses.  A router may maximize
revenue $R$, a user-facing generalized cost, or a constrained combination.  We
never infer which objective is deployed from public prices.

\section{Conditional Mechanism Results}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{Inverse-power routing is a benchmark, not a measurement}

Suppose, only for this subsection, that all $n$ providers are eligible and the
router really allocates
\begin{equation}
s_i(p)=\frac{p_i^{-a}}{\sum_jp_j^{-a}},\qquad a>0.
\label{eq:inversepower}
\end{equation}
With constant marginal cost $c_i$ and fixed demand, provider profit is
$\pi_i=(p_i-c_i)s_i$.  This is a useful mechanism benchmark even though our data
reject it as the full realized allocation law.

\begin{proposition}[Conditional share elasticity and markup]
Under \eqref{eq:inversepower},
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial\log s_i}{\partial\log p_i}=-a(1-s_i).
\end{equation}
Every interior best response satisfies the Lerner index
$L_i=(p_i-c_i)/p_i=1/[a(1-s_i)]>1/a$.  If costs are symmetric, an interior
symmetric equilibrium exists when $a(n-1)>n$ and has
\begin{equation}
p^*=c\frac{a(n-1)}{a(n-1)-n}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

The result explains why the routing exponent controls price incentives
\emph{when the mechanism is actually used}.  It does not license substituting
$a=2$ into a structural market model.  Private eligibility and quality change
both $s_i$ and the derivative providers face.

\subsection{Information congestion and the critical adaptive set}

The elasticity result is individual: it says how one provider's share changes
with its own quote.  It does not say how many providers should adapt to the same
market signal.  Let $\Sigma_n$ be the covariance matrix of the information used
by $n$ potentially adaptive providers and define its effective rank
\begin{equation}
r_n=\frac{(\operatorname{tr}\Sigma_n)^2}
{\operatorname{tr}(\Sigma_n^2)}\in[1,n].
\label{eq:effectiverank}
\end{equation}
Low $r_n$ means that many displayed responses are transformations of few common
signals; it does not imply communication.

\begin{assumption}[Reduced-form information congestion]
\label{ass:congestion}
If $k\in[0,n]$ providers adapt, their gross allocation benefit is $bk/n$ and
their social congestion loss is
\begin{equation}
C_n(k)=c\left(\frac{k}{n}\right)^2
\left(\frac{k}{r_n}\right)^\gamma,
\qquad b,c,\gamma>0.
\label{eq:congestioncost}
\end{equation}
The planner's reduced-form value is $V_n(k)=bk/n-C_n(k)$.
\end{assumption}

The first term is the local market-share opportunity.  The second encodes a
common-mode externality: correlated learners chase the same residual, overload
the same capacity state, or repeatedly exploit the same scoring error.  Its
form is an assumption, not an implication of inverse-power routing.

\begin{theorem}[Critical adaptive set]
\label{thm:congestion}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:congestion}, the unique continuous interior optimum,
before clipping to $[0,n]$, is
\begin{equation}
k_n^\circ=
\left(\frac{b n r_n^\gamma}{c(2+\gamma)}\right)^{1/(1+\gamma)}.
\label{eq:kstar}
\end{equation}
The integer optimum is one of $\lfloor k_n^\circ\rfloor$ and
$\lceil k_n^\circ\rceil$.  If $r_n\asymp n^\beta$ for
$\beta\in[0,1]$, then
\begin{equation}
k_n^*=\Theta\!\left(n^{(1+\gamma\beta)/(1+\gamma)}\right).
\label{eq:kstarscaling}
\end{equation}
Consequently, $k_n^*/n\to0$ when $\beta<1$, while $k_n^*=\Theta(n)$
when $\beta=1$.
\end{theorem}

The theorem separates two statements that are easy to conflate.  A provider
can have a large local return to undercutting while the efficient adaptive set
is a vanishing fraction.  Conversely, a common pricing response is not costly
when it reflects linearly growing independent information.  The sufficient
statistic is signal rank relative to market size, not the count of low quotes.

\begin{corollary}[Robust exposure cap]
Let $\Omega$ be a declared uncertainty set for $(b,c,\gamma,r_n)$.  The cap
\begin{equation}
\bar k_n=\min_{\omega\in\Omega}\lfloor k_n^\circ(\omega)\rfloor
\end{equation}
lies weakly below the increasing portion of $V_n(k;\omega)$ for every
$\omega\in\Omega$.  It prevents overexposure under all declared models, at the
cost of possibly excluding beneficial adaptation.
\end{corollary}

Operationally, the cap is a bound on correlated exposure, not on provider
entry.  A router can preserve exploration and redundancy while limiting the
aggregate attempt probability assigned to providers whose price innovations
share a factor.  The empirical burden is substantial: a cap is justified only
after effective rank transports to a holdout and the congestion loss is
calibrated with realized outcomes.

\subsection{Displayed price and latent score}

Let $\alpha_i$ denote a stable reduced-form non-price score on a fixed request
class and exact eligible menu.  The score-adjusted rule is
\begin{equation}
s_i(p,\alpha)=
\frac{p_i^{-a}e^{\alpha_i}}{\sum_j p_j^{-a}e^{\alpha_j}}.
\label{eq:pricescore}
\end{equation}
The parameter is deliberately not called quality.  It bundles quality, latency,
health, capacity, eligibility mismatch, and any persistent router preference
not represented by public price.

\begin{proposition}[Score--price equivalence and elasticity]
\label{prop:scoreprice}
Under \eqref{eq:pricescore}, define
$p_i^{\rm eff}=p_i\exp(-\alpha_i/a)$.  Then
$s_i(p,\alpha)=s_i(p^{\rm eff},0)$ and, for any two providers with positive
conditional selection probability,
\begin{equation}
\alpha_i-\alpha_j
=\log\frac{s_i}{s_j}+a\log\frac{p_i}{p_j}.
\label{eq:relativealpha}
\end{equation}
If $\alpha_i=h_i(\log p_i)$ while rival scores and prices are locally fixed,
\begin{equation}
-\frac{\partial\log s_i}{\partial\log p_i}
=(1-s_i)\{a-h_i'(\log p_i)\}.
\label{eq:scoreelasticity}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

Equation~\eqref{eq:scoreelasticity} is the mechanism object.  A score that
falls with an aggressive cut has $h_i'>0$ and attenuates price-induced share
capture; a score that rewards the same cut beyond its explicit price term has
$h_i'<0$ and amplifies it.  Equation~\eqref{eq:relativealpha} identifies a
relative level wedge from repeated owned choices conditional on exact menus and
a frozen $a$.  It does not identify the derivative without score movement or a
rule intervention.

For a below-benchmark provider, let $p^{(b,i)}$ replace only $p_i$ by the
model-author benchmark.  Define the unilateral price-induced share gain and its
score interaction as
\begin{align}
\Delta_i(\alpha)&=s_i(p,\alpha)-s_i(p^{(b,i)},\alpha),\\
\Gamma_i&=\Delta_i(\alpha)-\Delta_i(0).
\label{eq:interaction}
\end{align}
Negative $\Gamma_i$ means scoring attenuates the share benefit of undercutting;
positive $\Gamma_i$ means it amplifies it.  These one-provider counterfactuals
are not additive when several providers undercut simultaneously.

\subsection{The cost of omitting quality}

Let the generalized execution cost of provider $i$ be
\begin{equation}
g_i=a\log p_i+\lambda_L\widehat L_i+\lambda_F\widehat r_i
-\lambda_Q\widehat q_i-\lambda_K\log \widehat K_i,
\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}
where $\widehat r_i$ is failure risk and $\widehat K_i$ is committed, not merely
advertised, capacity.  For $x$ in the probability simplex $\Delta_n$, define the
entropy-regularized objective
\begin{equation}
F_g(x)=\langle x,g\rangle+\eta^{-1}\sum_i x_i\log x_i,
\qquad \eta>0.
\label{eq:gibbsobjective}
\end{equation}
Its minimizer is $x_i^g\propto e^{-\eta g_i}$.  A price-only router instead uses
$\tilde g_i=a\log p_i$ and $x^{\tilde g}$.

\begin{theorem}[Exact omitted-attribute regret]
\label{thm:kl}
For any finite score vector $g$ and any allocation $x\in\Delta_n$,
\begin{equation}
F_g(x)-F_g(x^g)=\eta^{-1}\KL(x\,\|\,x^g).
\label{eq:klidentity}
\end{equation}
In particular, the loss from price-only routing is exactly
$\eta^{-1}\KL(x^{\tilde g}\|x^g)$ and is zero if and only if the two allocations
coincide.  Omitted attributes that are constant across eligible providers do no
harm; attributes that reorder providers generally do.
\end{theorem}

The identity is sharper than saying quality ``may matter.''  It turns the
distance between a price-only and full score into an exact objective gap for a
declared regularized objective.  It also gives a measurable target: a router can
publish both score components and report the implied KL gap without disclosing
prompt contents.

\begin{theorem}[Robust approximate scoring]
\label{thm:robust}
Suppose $\|\widehat g-g\|_\infty\le\epsilon$ and let $x^{\widehat g}$ minimize
$F_{\widehat g}$.  Then
\begin{equation}
0\le F_g(x^{\widehat g})-F_g(x^g)\le 2\epsilon.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

Thus an auditable score can be approximately efficient without perfectly
estimating every attribute.  The guarantee is pointwise and does not assume a
provider behavioral model.  It does require that the score target $g$ encode the
planner or user objective rather than an ad hoc proxy.

\subsection{Why public prices cannot recover hidden capacity}

\begin{theorem}[Quote-only hidden-capacity impossibility]
\label{thm:impossibility}
Consider two eligible providers with identical observable quotes, public quality,
and public reliability.  There exist two economies consistent with those
observables: in economy $A$, provider 1 has committed capacity and social cost
$c$ while provider 2 has scarcity cost $c+\Delta$; in economy $B$ the costs are
reversed.  Any quote-only randomized router incurs expected cost regret at least
$\Delta/2$ in one economy.  A deterministic quote-only router incurs regret
$\Delta$ in one economy.
\end{theorem}

The theorem formalizes the reserved-capacity problem.  Fundraising, data-center
ownership, or a provider name is not the missing statistic: those are noisy
proxies for deliverable capacity.  The mechanism needs a commitment whose breach
has a consequence.  A capacity certificate can be a finite-slot report backed
by collateral, an execution-contingent price, or an SLA with measurable credits.

\subsection{Fee conflict}

Suppose the router receives an ad-valorem fee $\tau$ on selected spend.  Holding
volume and allocation fixed, its revenue is $\tau\sum_i x_ip_i$, while the user's
payment is $\sum_ix_ip_i$.  A common price increase therefore benefits the
router and harms the user one-for-one up to $\tau$.  A flat fee per successful
request is locally neutral to the selected price but can still distort admission
or volume.  Fee separation does not solve mechanism design, but it removes the
most direct price-level conflict.

\section{Data and Property Tests}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Capture system and estimands}

The main public panel begins on 7 July 2026 and, in the frozen release used
here, runs through 22 July.  It captures endpoint and model state at five-minute
cadence in a remote GitHub Actions--Hugging Face pipeline.  The immutable
revision \texttt{f4023bdad64b6ad468741a5a3b1f3afb7af40dd0} contains 3,182,780
endpoint observations, 3,274 snapshots, 321 model identifiers, and 72 provider
labels.  We
construct completion-price events only when a same endpoint-model quote changes
between valid snapshots.  We separately collect router-published rate-limit,
derank, and capacity fields.  Assignment-first paid probes record treatment
before sending a minimal request and retrieve the selected provider and outcome
for our account.  Private prompts and request rows never enter public releases.

Every result freezes a dataset revision and declares a calibration/holdout
split.  The information-congestion audit trains through 16 July 13:52 UTC and
evaluates later snapshots.  A clean integrity rerun executes 29 registered
public modules with zero failures and explicitly excludes prospective owned
outcomes.  These windows are short; they support falsification and mechanism
screening, not a mature structural estimate.

The primary price and competition population is paid-only.  We exclude model
IDs ending in \texttt{:free}, the \texttt{openrouter/free} meta-route, and
non-positive completion quotes; paid suffixes such as speed- or price-priority
routes are not excluded merely because their IDs contain a colon.  Free-tier
flow remains a separate subsidy/demand-creation estimand.  In this frozen
revision, 14 of 338 catalog entries are free-labelled and free routes account
for 10.9\% of tokens on the latest complete activity day, but zero of the 766
positive completion-price events.  Thus the paid filter changes demand totals,
not the focal pricing-event results.  Source-defined ``Other'' aggregates and
app-wide rankings lack variant identity and are not claimed to be cleanly
filterable.

\subsection{Regimes are persistent, except active undercutting}

Provider-model pairs are frozen in a training window into four descriptive
pricing regimes relative to the model-author benchmark: premium, anchor adopter,
static discounter, and active undercutter.  The names describe a price path, not
a provider's cost or intent.  Of 366 training pairs, 358 are holdout-evaluable.
Overall persistence is 0.944 (95\% Wilson interval $[0.915,0.964]$).  Premium,
static-discount, and anchor regimes persist at 0.992, 0.976, and 1.000,
respectively; active undercutting persists in only 5 of 21 pairs, or 0.238
($[0.106,0.451]$).  Fourteen of the 21 active pairs become static discounters.

This pattern fits a Brown--MacKay-style world in which repricing technologies
differ \cite{brownmackay2023}, but it does not identify such technology.  Active
undercutting could also reflect promotion windows, capacity shocks, or menu
corrections.  The testable implication is duration: future data should estimate
transition hazards as a function of benchmark gap, capacity state, and update
frequency, with provider-model fixed effects.

\subsection{The displayed-price transfer surface}
\label{sec:pricesurface}

We evaluate a 1,000-input-token, 256-output-token request at all 3,274 snapshots.
A provider is ``active'' only if it changes price at least twice before the
temporal split and its median quote lies below the author/menu benchmark.  The
classifier selects Novita, StreamLake, and Inceptron for GLM-5.2: three providers
in a median menu of 25.  For each post-split snapshot, we reset a nested active
set to the benchmark and recompute the public inverse-price surface.

The one-, two-, and three-provider sets receive 11.36\%, 22.81\%, and 27.61\%
of shadow share.  Their benchmark-reset counterfactuals are 2.64\%, 5.82\%, and
8.99\%, so the implied transfers from passive providers are 8.72, 16.99, and
18.62 percentage points.  These are exact rule effects conditional on the
public menu.  They are not realized flow or a demand estimate.

For GLM-5.2 the fitted effective-rank exponent is 0.967 with block-bootstrap
interval $[0.516,1.219]$ at one hour.  The six- and 24-hour estimates are 1.098
$[0.714,1.317]$ and 1.121 $[0.809,1.326]$.  The requested $k^*=o(n)$ reading
therefore does not follow.  Across 70 non-GLM paid markets, the cross-market
active-count exponent is 0.219 $[0.068,0.378]$ and 90\% have zero active
providers.  The only supported non-GLM rank ladder has an in-sample common
factor that collapses in the temporal holdout.  Current non-GLM data are sparse,
not evidence for $k=\Omega(n)$.

\subsection{Prospective identification of scoring beyond price}

The GLM-5.2 paid campaign freezes two default-route requests, one explicit
price-sort request, pins, and exclusion controls in each 15-minute block.  A
prospective amendment begins at 22:00 UTC on 21 July, after four earlier default
choices were observed; those four choices are excluded from this analysis.
Conditional multinomial choice estimates \eqref{eq:pricescore} with $a=1.6483$
and a fixed ridge penalty.  It reports relative $\alpha_i$ versus Z.AI, the
price-equivalent discount $1-e^{-\alpha_i/a}$, whole-block cluster intervals,
and the mean within-menu total variation between price-only and score-adjusted
probabilities.  Five-fold whole-block log-loss improvement measures information
beyond price in bits per choice; a conditional Monte Carlo test holds every menu
and quote fixed.

The interaction in \eqref{eq:interaction} is the primary economic output.  The
within-block price-sort arm separately estimates how explicitly changing the
routing rule affects cheapest-provider selection.  Diagnostic estimates require
40 covered prospective choices, 20 blocks, and three selected providers;
paper-strength interpretation requires 800 choices, 100 blocks, seven days, and
90\% candidate coverage.  The current aggregate has 36 covered default choices
in 18 blocks over 0.647 days.  Its public-price exponent is 1.735 with profile
interval $[0.31,3.16]$.  The focal active pair is selected 2/36 times, versus a
13.60\% frozen-rule prediction; the observed 5.56\% share has Wilson interval
$[1.54\%,18.14\%]$.  The score model has only 30 prospective choices in 15
blocks and is not fit.

The direct rule contrast is sharper.  In 15 complete blocks, default routing
selects the cheapest provider 0\% of the time and explicit price sorting does so
80\% of the time; the block-bootstrap interval for the 80-point difference is
$[60,100]$.  This owned-request effect shows that rule choice matters and that
default routing is not price-only.  It does not reveal the proprietary score or
market-wide flow, and remains accruing under the registered gate.

\subsection{No detectable excess rival response in the active regime}

For 1,052 active-undercutter events in 11 target provider-model clusters, the
registered rival-action rate is 0.455 ($[0.425,0.486]$), compared with 0.453
($[0.424,0.484]$) in a shifted timing placebo.  The paired difference is
$0.0019$; a target-cluster bootstrap interval is $[-0.0438,0.0410]$, and the
exact one-sided test for \emph{more} registered-window response has $p=0.468$.
The placebo is not randomized treatment.  The result places the immediate
response mechanism near zero at this cadence but does not exclude slower,
state-dependent, or provider-specific response.

\subsection{Displayed score is not realized clearing}

An independent welfare audit compares the elasticity implied by the public
inverse-square score with the observed aggregate share elasticity.  The
inverse-square restriction is rejected with $z=10.06$.  An earlier aggregate
estimate is $-1.19$ (standard error 0.17), but it is neither request-level
selection nor a price experiment.  We therefore use inverse-power routing only
as the conditional benchmark in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

Owned routing reinforces the distinction.  In the provider-regime release, 173
delegated selections match frozen regimes: 81 anchor and 92 premium, with no
current delegated support for the active or static regimes.  These counts
depend on our model and prompt mix and are not market shares.  A separate frozen
first-position experiment, H81, decomposes
fallback from delegated selection.  In its 133 preterminal assignments, the
missing-outcome identified set places the fallback and total-delegation contrast
at 4.4--6.7 percentage points and hidden selection at zero.  Missingness
suppresses the complete-data primary randomization family, and two repeated
models prevent broad transport.  The fixed-horizon H95 replication remains
blinded until its preregistered horizon and is not pooled with H81.

\subsection{Unit share elasticity is not a revenue first-order condition}

The pooled public quantity-share regression gives a price coefficient of
$-1.104$ (clustered standard error 0.079), statistically equivalent to $-1$
within the registered 0.25 margin.  That resemblance is not a residual-demand
derivative.  The within-provider/model coefficient is $-0.605$ (0.196), while
the between coefficient is $-1.184$ (0.195); the between-minus-within gap is
$-0.579$ with $p=0.0031$.  Only 68 listed-price events are available, 10.5\% of
listed entities vary price, and within-price variation fails the promotion gate.

There is also an exact accounting explanation.  Let $s_i$ be token share,
$e_i=p_iq_i/\sum_jp_jq_j$ be public price-times-token revenue share, and
$P=\sum_jp_jq_j/\sum_jq_j$.  Within a model-day market,
\begin{equation}
\log s_i=\log e_i-\log p_i+\log P.
\label{eq:revenueidentity}
\end{equation}
The revenue-share price coefficient is $-0.104$ (0.079), so the quantity-share
coefficient is exactly one lower.  The identity holds to
$3.6\times10^{-15}$.  It explains why a pooled slope near $-1$ can coexist with
price-neutral revenue shares; it does not explain endogenous price--quantity
matching or identify profit maximization.

A bounded unilateral counterfactual places the observed proxy-weighted provider
revenue gap at 1.87--1.97\% under the registered demand scenarios, with a
combined bootstrap and elasticity envelope of 1.65--2.03\%.  This is conditional
on treating the public elasticity as causal and extending it with a logit curve.
Router revenue is not point identified because the applicable take rate,
end-user price, and market demand curve are unobserved.

\subsection{Quality, capacity, and ``dumping'' remain unidentified}

Premium providers do not show a significant holdout throughput advantage
($p=0.507$) or latency advantage ($p=0.796$) in provider-model residual
aggregates.  This does not prove premiums lack value: task fidelity, privacy,
support, and selection into quality measurement remain missing.  More sharply,
zero provider-model cells have a valid utilization measure linking load to a
capacity ceiling.  Static discounters more often fall below one scenario GPU-
cost bound, but marginal cost, reserved-capacity amortization, rebates, and
subsidies are unobserved.  The registered verdict is therefore
\texttt{dumping\_supported=false}.

The same caution applies to geography.  A name-based ``China-linked'' tag is
more common among active and static discount regimes, but a name tag is neither
cost nor strategy.  A causal explanation requires at least location, hardware,
energy price, reserved capacity, subsidy/rebate exposure, and realized
utilization.  These are fields for a data partnership, not coefficients to
impute from names.

\subsection{Dynamic score memory is outside the identified mechanism}

The estimand in this paper is contemporaneous: conditional on the current menu,
how much does displayed price move the declared allocation rule, and how much
does the current reduced-form score attenuate or amplify that movement?  Neither
the public panel nor the owned-choice design identifies a law of motion for the
score.  In particular, persistence of a provider's quote is not evidence that a
router remembers the quote, and a lagged provider response is not evidence that
the router carries a hidden penalty state.

We therefore remove critical-memory and signal-coupling simulations from this
paper's evidentiary chain.  They remain separate learning benchmarks.  A dynamic
score model can enter this mechanism paper only after lagged price and quality
histories improve held-out prediction of owned routing choices beyond the
current menu, provider--model effects, enforcement state, and contemporaneous
quality, and after a randomized router or partner-log design distinguishes
score memory from unobserved eligibility.  Until then, $\alpha_{it}$ is a
current composite residual, not a state variable with an estimated transition.

\section{An Auditable Router Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Score, commitments, and exploration}

Theorems~\ref{thm:kl}--\ref{thm:impossibility} suggest a concrete design.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item Compute a generalized score like \eqref{eq:score}, with task-conditional
latency, failure, and quality predictions estimated out of sample.
\item Replace advertised capacity with an execution-contingent commitment:
finite slots, collateral, or SLA credits.  Publish how retries enter score and
payment.
\item Use bounded exploration so new or small providers can generate evidence;
cap concentration and common-mode exposure.
\item Charge the router through a fee separated from selected spend, while
reporting both user generalized cost and router revenue.
\end{enumerate}

The score need not collapse every objective into one undocumented number.  The
router can publish a Pareto frontier over user generalized cost, router revenue,
provider viability, concentration, and completion.  Different user classes can
choose points on that frontier, provided the accounting is explicit.

\subsection{Three objective vectors, three mechanisms}

The same evidence can support several explicit objectives without pretending
they coincide.  Let $x$ be delivered allocation and let $q_i,r_i,L_i,c_i$ denote
verified quality, failure risk, latency, and resource cost:
\begin{align}
G_U(x)&=\sum_i x_i[p_i+\lambda_LL_i+\lambda_Fr_i-v(q_i)],\\
G_W(x)&=\sum_i x_i[c_i+\lambda_LL_i+\lambda_Fr_i-v(q_i)],\\
G_R(x)&=-\sum_i x_i[f_i(p_i)-C_{\mathrm{retry},i}-C_{\mathrm{SLA},i}].
\label{eq:threeobjectives}
\end{align}
An entropy-regularized router can minimize any one by using its corresponding
score.  The user-cost and welfare rules differ because price is a transfer in
$G_W$ but a cost in $G_U$; an ad-valorem revenue rule may rank a high price above
a low price; and a quality-first rule can rationally buy expensive execution
when verified fidelity is valuable.  None dominates without value, cost, and
participation restrictions.

Our implementation recommendation is a declared Pareto menu, not one secret
scalar: (i) a user generalized-cost arm, (ii) a resilience-constrained welfare
arm using cost bounds and capacity commitments, and (iii) a quality-priority arm
with a spend cap.  Router revenue is reported as an outcome or constrained
floor, not silently inserted into the user score.  Provider entry, investment,
and best responses are recomputed for each arm in simulation; the live
experiment randomizes only arms that pass completion and fidelity safety floors.

\subsection{Robust policy optimization}

Let $\theta$ parameterize score coefficients, exploration, caps, and fee design.
Let $\Omega$ be a declared set of cost, quality-value, capacity, and provider-
learning models consistent with the evidence.  One implementable objective is
\begin{align}
\min_{\theta}\ \sup_{\omega\in\Omega}\;&
\mathcal R_U(\theta;\omega)
+\lambda_P\max_i\mathcal E_i(\theta;\omega)
+\lambda_C\max_{S:|S|\le k}\mathcal E_S(\theta;\omega) \nonumber\\
&+\lambda_V\mathcal V(\theta;\omega),
\label{eq:robustdesign}
\end{align}
subject to completion, concentration, budget, and capacity constraints.
$\mathcal R_U$ is user regret relative to the best feasible full-information
policy; $\mathcal E_i$ and $\mathcal E_S$ are unilateral and coalition
exploitability; and $\mathcal V$ collects constraint violations.  This is not a
claim that the router knows welfare.  It is a way to report which uncertainties
drive a design recommendation.

\subsection{Structural equilibrium and objective frontier}

We construct ten declared provider technologies: an author, two anchor
resellers, two providers with reserved capacity, three spot-dependent startups,
and two custom-quality providers.  Technologies differ in marginal and fixed
cost, capacity, reliability, latency, and quality.  Anchors hold the benchmark
price; the other providers play cyclic bounded price best responses.  We screen
108 router rules over price exponent, quality and reliability weights, uniform
exploration, and a cap on providers assigned to one correlated-pricer group.
Payments cancel in welfare.  Every selected rule reports unilateral grid regret
and a same-type two-provider joint-deviation screen.

\subsection{The policy-frontier experiment}

The structural frontier suggests a prospective experiment whose estimands are narrower than
``welfare'' but decision-relevant.  Randomize request triplets within
model--prompt--time blocks among (i) the production delegated policy, (ii) a
price-ordered policy with fallback, and (iii) the adaptive generalized score.
Freeze the adaptive score before opening outcomes.  The primary vector is
attempted-request spend, completion, total latency including retries, and a
blinded task-fidelity score.  Provider choice, fallback position, and public
enforcement state are mechanism variables rather than outcomes to condition on.

Because routing treatments can change congestion, inference should randomize
time clusters or use switchbacks after a low-load pilot, report assignment-first
intention-to-treat effects, and preserve bounded-outcome randomization intervals.
No scalar index should be primary unless the user-value weights are frozen in
advance.  Instead, report the joint Pareto set and the range of value weights for
which each policy is preferred.  A second, provider-facing phase randomizes
execution-contingent capacity commitments among willing endpoints and measures
admission, delivery, and post-experiment retention.  This separates the short-run
routing effect from the longer-run entry and investment response that a menu
replay cannot recover.

The experiment has three promotion gates.  First, the adaptive arm must preserve
randomization integrity and overlap across models, hours, and provider sets.
Second, its completion and fidelity lower bounds must exceed declared safety
floors.  Third, any welfare statement must remain valid over a preregistered box
of user values and provider cost bounds.  Failure of the third gate still permits
a useful multi-outcome mechanism comparison; it only blocks scalar welfare.

\subsection{Practical provider types}

The mechanism must work across three economic technologies even though public
price regimes do not identify them.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
\item \emph{Owned or reserved capacity}: high fixed cost, low short-run marginal
cost, and a strong incentive to maintain utilization.  Capacity commitments are
credible when backed by sunk infrastructure but still require outage penalties.
\item \emph{Spot-dependent providers}: flexible entry and state-dependent cost.
They provide resilience but may disappear exactly during common scarcity.
State-contingent commitment prices are more appropriate than a fixed share.
\item \emph{Model authors and anchor adopters}: an author quote can coordinate
expectations, while identical third-party quotes may reflect default menus rather
than common costs.  The router should not treat an anchor as a cost certificate.
\end{itemize}

Free entry is not automatically efficient.  An entrant adds a redundant delivery
path but diverts requests and may increase retry or monitoring costs, echoing the
classic business-stealing result \cite{mankiw1986freeentry}.  Capacity-certified
entry is preferable to counting endpoint labels: it rewards incremental delivered
capacity rather than duplicated menus.

\section{Related Work}

Multi-model routers such as FrugalGPT, RouteLLM, and RouterBench optimize across
models \cite{chen2023frugalgpt,ong2024routellm,hu2024routerbench}.  We hold the
model label fixed and study provider procurement; the two routing layers compose.
Multidimensional procurement shows why price and quality must be scored jointly
\cite{che1993}; two-sided platform theory clarifies fee and participation
externalities \cite{rochettirole2003}.

Brown and MacKay study heterogeneous pricing technology and repricing frequency
\cite{brownmackay2023}.  Their framework motivates our duration and regime
transition tests, not a provider-type label.  Hansen, Misra, and Pai show that
independent, misspecified pricing learners can produce supra-competitive prices
through correlated experiments \cite{hansenmisrapai2021}; our ordered property
chain and signal-order intervention isolate that mechanism and currently fail to
transport it.  Johnson, Rhodes, and Wildenbeest show how platform prominence can
change algorithmic competition \cite{johnsonrhodeswildenbeest2023}.  Inference
routing adds hidden eligibility, fallback, and stochastic delivered quality.

The closest empirical benchmark uses OpenRouter and Azure data to study the
emerging market for intelligence \cite{demireretal2025}.  We focus on provider-
level microstructure and the gap between displayed and delivered liquidity.
Switchback and marketplace experiments face interference
\cite{bojinov2023,holtz2025}; our assignment-first probes and first-position
estimands are designed around that problem.  Price focal points and the reflection
problem motivate hard nulls for anchor following rather than causal stories from
ties alone \cite{knittelstango2003,manski1993}.

\section{Limitations and Conclusion}

The public panel is measured in weeks, not quarters.  Owned probes cover one
account, region, workload, and budget.  Capacity, provider cost, user task value,
private rebates, and market-wide flow are not observed.  Public enforcement
fields are router-published aggregates rather than request logs.  The current
quality panel is selected and incomplete.  Public shadow rules hold eligibility
fixed; simulator interventions are causal only inside the simulator.  We therefore do
not estimate dumping, literal front-running, communication, collusion, or a scalar
welfare loss.

Within those limits, the market description changes sharply.  Open-weight
execution is not a transparent commodity exchange.  It is a two-layer
procurement market in which public prices sit above hidden admission, scoring,
and fallback.  The empirical evidence rejects several tempting simplifications:
inverse-square simulated share is not realized flow; active undercutting is not a
stable provider type; current response timing does not support a simple reactive
collusion story; pooled unit share elasticity is not an own-price first-order
condition; and premium prices are not yet explained by observed QoS.  It also
refuses the paper's most tempting positive contrast: current GLM rank is linear-
compatible and non-GLM active repricing is sparse, so neither proposed asymptotic
regime is empirically established.

The design response is not to declare a better exponent.  It is to expose the
missing contracting and information dimensions.  The critical-set theorem says
when correlated adaptation can require a minority exposure cap; the data say
that premise is not yet established.  Generalized scoring has an exact regret
interpretation, approximate scores have a robust guarantee, and hidden capacity
creates a formal impossibility for quote-only routing.  Execution-contingent
prices, covariance-aware caps, verified quality, bounded exploration, and fee
separation turn those results into testable mechanisms.  The next empirical
step is a randomized policy frontier with delivered outcomes and declared value
bands.  Until then, the honest output is a set of feasible mechanisms, not one
estimated welfare optimum.

\section{Proofs}

\paragraph{Conditional share elasticity and markup.}
Write $W_{-i}=\sum_{j\ne i}p_j^{-a}$.  Direct differentiation gives
\[
\frac{\partial s_i}{\partial p_i}
=-\frac{a}{p_i}s_i(1-s_i).
\]
The first-order condition for $(p_i-c_i)s_i$ is
$1-a(p_i-c_i)(1-s_i)/p_i=0$, yielding the Lerner expression.  At a
symmetric profile $s_i=1/n$; solving for $p$ gives the formula.  A finite positive
solution requires $a(n-1)>n$. \hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:congestion}.}
Substituting \eqref{eq:congestioncost} gives
\[
V_n(k)=\frac{bk}{n}
-\frac{c k^{2+\gamma}}{n^2r_n^\gamma}.
\]
For $k>0$ the second derivative is strictly negative.  The first-order
condition is
$b/n=c(2+\gamma)k^{1+\gamma}/(n^2r_n^\gamma)$, which yields
\eqref{eq:kstar}.  Strict concavity implies that the integer maximizer is one
of the adjacent integers.  Substituting $r_n\asymp n^\beta$ gives
\eqref{eq:kstarscaling}; dividing by $n$ proves the density statements.
\hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:scoreprice}.}
Substitution gives
$(p_i^{\rm eff})^{-a}=p_i^{-a}e^{\alpha_i}$, proving allocation
equivalence.  Taking the log odds of $i$ and $j$ yields
$\log(s_i/s_j)=-a\log(p_i/p_j)+\alpha_i-\alpha_j$ and hence
\eqref{eq:relativealpha}.  Finally differentiate
$\log s_i=-a\log p_i+h_i(\log p_i)-\log\sum_jp_j^{-a}e^{\alpha_j}$
with respect to $\log p_i$.  The direct term is $-a+h_i'$ and its contribution
to the log denominator is $s_i(-a+h_i')$, giving
$(1-s_i)(-a+h_i')$. \hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:kl}.}
The Gibbs minimizer satisfies $x_i^g=e^{-\eta g_i}/Z_g$, so
$g_i=-\eta^{-1}\log x_i^g-\eta^{-1}\log Z_g$.  Substitution into
\eqref{eq:gibbsobjective} gives
\[
F_g(x)=\eta^{-1}\sum_i x_i\log\frac{x_i}{x_i^g}
-\eta^{-1}\log Z_g.
\]
The second term equals $F_g(x^g)$, proving \eqref{eq:klidentity}.
\hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:robust}.}
For every $x\in\Delta_n$,
$|F_g(x)-F_{\widehat g}(x)|=|\langle x,g-\widehat g\rangle|\le\epsilon$.
Optimality of $x^{\widehat g}$ for $F_{\widehat g}$ therefore implies
\[
F_g(x^{\widehat g})\le F_{\widehat g}(x^{\widehat g})+\epsilon
\le F_{\widehat g}(x^g)+\epsilon\le F_g(x^g)+2\epsilon.
\]
The lower bound follows from optimality of $x^g$. \hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:impossibility}.}
A quote-only router must use the same allocation in both observationally
equivalent economies.  Let $x$ be the probability assigned to provider 1.  Its
regret is $\Delta(1-x)$ in economy $A$ and $\Delta x$ in economy $B$; the larger
is at least $\Delta/2$.  If $x\in\{0,1\}$, the larger is $\Delta$.
\hfill$\square$

\section{Evidence and Claim Ledger}

The critical-set result is a theorem conditional on
Assumption~\ref{ass:congestion}; SM3 validates its numerical scaling but its
bandit allocation effect includes zero.  WF20 uses 3,274 public snapshots and
identifies a finite-menu rank diagnostic plus a deterministic shadow rule.  It
does not identify asymptotic rank, realized share, $k^*$, provider learning, or
welfare.  GLM-5.2 is linear-compatible, and the non-GLM census does not support
linear-density active pricing.

The paid GLM panel identifies an owned-account effect of explicit price sorting
in 15 complete blocks.  It does not identify market-wide share, and its latent
score, duration, and choice gates remain open.  H91 and H92 establish a within--
between difference and an accounting identity, not causal residual demand.
WCV6 is a bounded sensitivity analysis.  H81 identifies a narrow fallback
decomposition, not a general router policy.  Residual QoS tests and zero valid
utilization cells do not identify technology rent, dumping, or marginal cost.

SM4 is a declared structural game.  Its objective frontier, unilateral grid
regret, technology stresses, and pairwise deviation gains are simulator objects.
No result in this paper identifies router score memory, provider learning memory,
communication, collusion, a provider algorithm, or live welfare.
