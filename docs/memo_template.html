Is the inference market DeFi-like? An empirical screen

Ten pre-specified hypothesis tests on OpenRouter's per-provider pricing, routing, and order-flow data, run before any theory. Verdict: not an AMM — an aggregated RFQ market: firm posted quotes (rejection is capacity, not quote-fading), quality-tiered pricing with a lemons tail, a zero-basis passthrough router with an internal maker scorecard, and free entry at the margin.

Data: OpenRouter capture (873 provider-endpoints, 2023–26 backfill) · vast.ai GPU offers Repo: pluriholonomic/ai-routing · HF: t4run/openrouter-market-history (private)

The deeper thing (v2, sharpened): OpenRouter is empirically an aggregated RFQ market with posted quotes. Providers quote like PMMs — quality-tiered pricing with a measurable lemons tail (H11), capacity-based rejection in the tail but no strategic quote-fading (H10: rejection uncorrelated with price aggressiveness, so quotes are firm), and the router passes maker quotes through at exactly face value (H13: zero basis on 128/128 matched models) while charging its take off-quote, like an RFQ aggregator and unlike an exchange. The AMM leg of the analogy fails cleanly (H1, H2, H6); the quote-driven OTC leg survives every test run so far. Theory target: search-and-bargaining (Duffie–Gârleanu–Pedersen) plus RFQ aggregation with a reliability-scored router — OpenRouter even maintains a Beta posterior (fortuna) over each quoter's fill reliability, an MM scorecard in production.

H6

Fees are structurally different — and listed prices overstate paid prices

skin-deep

OpenRouter's take (~5.5% credit fee) is 20–100× a DEX aggregator's (0–30 bps) — the search/switching friction it monetizes is simply larger than in crypto, consistent with H2's persistent dispersion. Separately, transacted prices sit below list: the median effective/listed input-price ratio is 0.93, but the tail is deep — the 10th percentile pair pays 39% of list, driven by prompt caching (median measured cache-hit rate 22%; heavy-agent workloads run far higher). This is the quoted-vs-effective-spread distinction from market microstructure, alive in inference.

Phase 2b — the RFQ refinement

V1 left "intent/solver market" standing; the sharper hypothesis is that OpenRouter is an aggregated RFQ market: providers as PMM-style quoters (with last look?), the router as an RFQ aggregator keeping a reliability scorecard, harness flow as preferenced order flow. Four tests ran same-day on data already captured.

H10

Rejection is rampant — but it is not phantom liquidity

partial

RFQ market makers post aggressive quotes and reject toxic fills ("last look"). The inference analog — rate limiting and derankable errors — is real and heavy-tailed: median endpoint rejects 1.5% of requests but the 90th percentile rejects 28%, and 32% of endpoints exceed the FX last-look norm (2–10%). The RFQ pathology, however, fails both of its tests: the price→reject slope is positive and insignificant (+0.03, p = 0.12) — cheap quotes fill as well as expensive ones — and reweighting dispersion by executability changes it by −0.3%. Rejection is capacity, not strategy: quotes are firm, and H2's dispersion is fully executable. OpenRouter runs a live maker scorecard regardless — a Beta posterior (fortuna) plus capacity_ceiling_rpm per endpoint — structurally identical to RFQ aggregators' fill-quality tiering.

H11

A lemons tail: cheap quotes deliver worse execution

deep

Extending the hedonic to delivered quality (tool-call error rate, structured-output error rate, throughput, latency) explains 31.9% of within-model price variance — six times what static specs explained in H2 — so a genuine quality frontier exists. And the tail is a lemons market: below-median-price endpoints deliver above-median tool-call error rates 55.8% of the time vs 37.9% for expensive ones (χ² p = 0.002). This is the microstructure distinction between quoted and realized execution quality, alive in inference. Quantization labels, by contrast, carry no clean discount — quality is priced through delivered metrics, not labels.

H12

The compute basis: on-demand vs interruptible GPUs

partial

vast.ai quotes the same GPU two ways — guaranteed (on-demand) and interruptible (bid) — and the spread is a funding-rate/convenience-yield analog for the industry's input. Latest ratios run 1.16× (RTX 3090) to 1.67× (H100 NVL), median 1.41× across classes. Measured comparator (Deribit BTC-PERPETUAL, 57k hourly points since 2020): median |8h funding| is 0.0024% ≈ 2.6%/yr — the same economic object (a premium for guaranteed continuous access) at roughly three orders of magnitude lower level. Compute's interruption premium is enormous because arbitraging it requires physical checkpoint/resume, not a funding trade. Dynamics (mean reversion, spikes) accrue with the hourly panel and feed H7's pass-through test.

H13

Venue basis: exactly zero — the router is a pure quote-passthrough broker

deep

For all 128 DeepInfra models matched between OpenRouter's quotes and DeepInfra's own public price list, the basis is 0.0% — to the digit. OpenRouter displays maker quotes verbatim and levies its 5.5% take off-quote (on credits), exactly how RFQ aggregators present MM quotes, and unlike exchange venues where cross-venue bases fluctuate and get arbitraged. Daily direct-provider capture now parses DeepInfra and Cerebras public model APIs, Groq and Together exact-ID catalogs, and a bounded set of Fireworks exact-ID model pages; Novita, Lambda, and Hyperbolic remain raw-archived only. The panel watches whether the basis ever deviates — e.g. whether OpenRouter lags provider repricing events, which would create transient bases like stale RFQ quotes.

H19

Provider typology: the market has quoter types, like a dealer market

deep

H18's dominant predictor was persistent seller heterogeneity, so we clustered the 71 consolidated provider families on 15 behavioral features (quote firmness, price aggressiveness, execution quality, catalog strategy, OR-volume). BIC's top split: firm quoters (day-0 listing, full precision, ~0% rejects, usually cheapest) vs soft quoters (quantized, ~8% rejects, ~6 weeks to list). Five sub-types emerge — and notably, off-OpenRouter-first businesses are identifiable purely behaviorally: they price high, almost never compete for cheapest, list late, and ration capacity (Baseten/Fireworks reject 37% of requests — a thin OR slice of a large direct business).

H17

What triggers repricing: experimentation and reaction, not costs

mechanism

Live event log (5-min resolution, day one n=11 — case evidence): 73% of events hit models under 30 days old (median age 21 days), 55% were reversed within the day, 45% followed a competitor's move on the same model within 48h, 27% were provider-wide waves, and movers averaged 2.3× their capacity ceiling in the 6h before moving. Three years of historical changes (n≈1,040, Wayback) add the lifecycle: month-one changes are 66% cuts with median 31% moves (launch premium decaying), falling monotonically to 38% cuts for models over a year old. Cross-provider corroboration from the LiteLLM community price archive (H1b: 56k provider-level pairs, 2023-26, 95 providers) shows the same 66% cut share and an extreme mover/stayer split — most providers never reprice; a small managed-price class does all the moving.

H7

Cost pass-through: token prices are not anchored to GPU rents

skin-deep · preliminary

A chained matched-model token index (BLS-style, ~232 matched models/month, no survivor bias) fell −53.6% from 2023-07 to 2026-06 (≈ −22%/yr like-for-like). Over the same era, H100 rents fell −19% (hyperscaler) to −24% (marketplace) per Silicon Data's published medians. Tokens deflate 2–3× faster than the input — the hashprice-style cost-anchor story fails at first pass; the majority of token deflation is serving efficiency and competition. Period-level correlations are underpowered (4–6 periods/segment, unstable signs) — the daily ECM version runs automatically as the vast.ai and 5-min token panels accumulate, and commercial index series (Bloomberg SDH100RT, OCPI-H100) drop into data-static/gpu_index_periods.csv if subscribed.

Executable cash-and-carry for a non-storable commodity: short the 1-yr forward, deliver by rolling spot purchases. The book marks nightly.
  • H37 · Inventory-control forensics (live 2026-07-08). Which capacity policy do providers run? Four classical tests: (a) staffing: buffer–load slope 0.83 ± 0.07 (n=253) — proportional overcapacity (ED regime), not call-center square-root staffing — GPU capacity is lumpy and slack is held in proportion to load; (b) Erlang-B inversion: implied capacity ÷ fortuna ceiling median 0.96 (n=416) — observed blocking is quantitatively consistent with an Erlang loss system AND independently validates the router's capacity estimates that the H34 order book rests on; (c) latency–load elasticity −0.10 ± 0.03 (269 endpoints, 61k ticks) — batching economies, not congestion: under normal load, more traffic slightly improves latency, confirming the flat-supply-curve-until-the-cliff premise behind H30; (d) Hawkes counting ratio 1.45 children/event within 24h (n=11, upper bound) — the repricing stream is strongly self-exciting (the war cascade), exponential-kernel MLE gates at 100 events. Synthesis: proportional-overcapacity inventory + bid-price-style admission (rejects track flow quality, not price — H24), with congestion invisible except in tail events.
  • H38 · Demand burstiness (live 2026-07-08). Three timescales, three instruments: 30-min Fano factor median ≈ 1,000 (Poisson = 1; a lower bound given rolling-window smoothing) — demand is massively overdispersed, nothing like independent arrivals; within-endpoint 30-min CV median 0.45 (p90 1.09) with only 2.5% of variance diurnal (short panel + UTC pooling caveat) — bursts, not cycles; sub-window peaks reach 37× average (p90 ≈ 2×) per fortuna's instantaneous peak-rpm; and daily model demand has CV 0.46 with 60% of models seeing a ≥2× day within a month. This rationalizes the whole inventory stack: proportional overcapacity (H37a), bid-price rationing (H24), and ceilings exceeded 5–10× at events (H16) are what serving Fano-1000 flow requires.
  • H39 · Arrival-process modeling (live 2026-07-08, preliminary at 22h of panel). No raw timestamps exist — counts through 30-min windows — so count-native point-process fits: Poisson-INGARCH(1,1) persistence median 0.75, p90 0.91, 13% of endpoints near-critical (>0.9) — the count analog of a Hawkes branching ratio says demand itself is strongly self-exciting; and aggregated-variance Hurst median 0.84 (IQR 0.75–0.91) — self-similar traffic, squarely in the classic Ethernet-traffic range (Leland/Taqqu), confirming H38's scale-invariant CV.
